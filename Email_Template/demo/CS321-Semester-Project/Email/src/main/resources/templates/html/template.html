Email Writing Tips

Click on each section below to learn more about writing the perfect email!

Introduction

Writing a clear, professional email can make all the difference when reaching out to professors, potential employers, or colleagues. This guide will walk you through the essential parts of an email, how to write it effectively, and help you get started with your email templates.

Parts of an Email

  • Subject Line: Short, specific, and to the point.
  • Greeting: Address the recipient respectfully (e.g., "Dear Dr. Mourad").
  • Introduction: State your purpose clearly in the first sentence.
  • Body: Provide context and details concisely.
  • Call to Action: Specify what you need or expect from the recipient.
  • Closing: Use a polite sign-off (e.g., "Best regards").
  • Signature: Include your name and, if applicable, contact information.

Writing Emails Professionally

  • Be concise and avoid unnecessary details.
  • Use proper grammar, punctuation, and spelling.
  • Avoid overly casual language (e.g., "Hey" or "LOL").
  • Always proofread before sending.
  • Maintain a professional tone, even if the email is casual.

Examples of Professional Emails

1. Academic Inquiry

Subject: Request for Appointment

Dear Professor Mourad,
I hope this email finds you well. I am writing to request an appointment to discuss my research proposal for the upcoming semester. Please let me know your availability next week, and I will do my best to accommodate your schedule.

Thank you for your time and guidance.
Best regards,
Jane Doe
(Student ID: 123456789)

2. Job Application Follow-Up

Subject: Follow-Up on Job Application

Dear Mr. Duong,
I hope you're doing well. I am following up on my application for the Software Engineer position submitted on October 25th. I wanted to kindly inquire if there are any updates on the hiring process. I remain very enthusiastic about the opportunity to contribute to your team and would be happy to provide any additional information if needed.

Thank you for your time, and I look forward to hearing from you.
Sincerely,
John Smith

3. Networking Introduction

Subject: Introduction - [Your Name]

Hi Mr. Nguyen,
My name is Emily Davis, and I am a recent graduate in Marketing from George Mason University. I came across your profile on LinkedIn and was impressed by your work at XYZ Agency. I am eager to learn more about your experiences and would appreciate the opportunity to connect or schedule a brief call at your convenience.

Thank you for your time, and I look forward to hearing from you.
Warm regards,
Emily Davis

Questions to Ask Yourself

  • What is the purpose of this email?
  • Who is the recipient, and how formal should my tone be?
  • What key details need to be included?
  • What do I want the recipient to do after reading this email?
  • Is this the best form of communication for this purpose?

Common Mistakes

  • Writing overly long emails that lack a clear purpose.
  • Using vague or generic subject lines.
  • Failing to proofread for typos or grammatical errors.
  • Including unnecessary or irrelevant details.
  • Using an inappropriate tone for the recipient or context.
  • Forgetting to include a clear call to action or next steps.
